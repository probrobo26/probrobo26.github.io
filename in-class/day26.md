## Broader Impacts
Creating "intelligent" robots (that use the power of probabilistic reasoning) introduces new facets for consideration at the time of design and deployment of these systems. Unlike other software, by virtue of being embodied, robots can literally change the physical world directly, and the relationships that embodied software can form with the physical world is distinct and unique. Robots are very much a _sociotechnology_ and we find them being embedded in various aspects of every day lives.

First, individually, please reflect on the following:
* Using the robot that you discussed in the last class, how do you _feel_ about this robot? What reaction does it elicit from you when you think about it? If it is a robot that you've interacted with before, what reaction did it elicit from you in real life? What is the root of this reaction?
* What use cases of robots excite you the most? The least? From these, develop at least two "axes" on which you might judge a robot system. 

Now, partnering with at least one other person:
* Share your axes for judging a robot system; brainstorm a list of at least 10 specific robots together and put them on these axes.

Take a gallery walk around the room and consider the other axes and robotic systems other groups listed. Add your own robots to some of these axes. 

As a group, let's discuss:
* Were there any emergent themes in the axes that people chose?
* Were there any disagreements you had with the rating of a robot, or did you tend to lean towards any axes/robots in particular?
* What responsibility does an engineer who chooses to work on any of these robots have with respect to the quality, deployment, social capacity, or cultural impact of the robot?
